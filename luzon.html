All About Luzon

The Philippines' biggest and most significant island is Luzon. It is the location of Quezon City and Manila, the nation's capital and largest metropolis. It is bordered by the South China Sea (west), the Philippine Sea (east), and the Sibuyan Sea (south) and is part of the Philippine archipelago (west). The Luzon Strait divides Taiwan from Luzon to the north. Luzon is the nation's leader in both agriculture and industry (which are both centered around Manila) (rice, corn [maize], coconuts, sugarcane, mangoes, bananas). The main grain-producing region is a central plain that stretches 100 miles (160 km) north of Manila. The stunning rice terraces built by the Ifugao mountaineers are located further north. The Bondoc and Bicol peninsulas both have sizable coconut plantations. Mining is done for copper, manganese, gold, and iron. Excellent hardwoods are produced in forests.

The principal cities are Pasay, Cabanatuan, Legaspi, Baguio, Batangas, and Laoag in addition to Manila and Quezon City. Luzon is home to more than half of the Filipino people. Dimensions: 40,420 square miles (104,688 square km). Pop. (2015) 57,470,097 people, including neighboring islands

Famous Tourist Spots

BATANES

At the meeting point of the Pacific Ocean and the South China Sea, the northernmost and smallest province of the nation, Batanes, is one of the most sought-after tourist frontier sites. The government has designated the archipelago province, known as "Nature's Playground," as a Protected Landscape and Seascape due to its uncommon flora and fauna, undulating terrain, variety of marine life, limestone cliffs, and expansive panoramas. It features lengths of crystal-clear water and white and cream sand beaches.




VIGAN, ILOCOS SUR

Vigan is an island that was formerly divided from the mainland by the enormous Abra River, the Mestizo River, and the Govantes River. It stands out among Filipino towns since it is the biggest and only historical city from the country's 16th-century Spanish colonial era still standing. Before the arrival of the colonizers, Vigan was a significant coastal commerce hub.




MAYON VOLCANO, ALBAY CITY

Mayon Volcano is an active volcano in southeastern Luzon, Philippines, that towers over the city of Legaspi. It has a base that is 80 miles (130 km) in circumference and rises to an elevation of 8,077 feet (2,462 meters) above the coasts of the Albay Gulf, earning it the moniker "most perfect volcanic cone in the world" due to the symmetry of its shape. The center of Mayon Volcano National Park is the volcano, which is well-liked by climbers and campers (21 square miles [55 square km]).

EL-NIDO, PALAWAN

The largest island in the Philippines is Palawan. Due to its arid half, the island is yet undeveloped. Enjoy the mountains, white beaches, and jungle. National Geographic Traveler magazine ranked Palawan Island as the 13th greatest island in the world and the best region in Southeast Asia in 2007! is the Philippines' most biodiverse island group. An archipelago, Palawan has 1,780 islands in its western portion. The island of Palawan is referred to as "The Last Ecological Frontier of the Philippines" due to its stunning landscapes and high biodiversity.

BAGUIO CITY (The Mansion)

One of the most visited attractions in Baguio City is The Mansion. Starting with Manuel Quezon, this has been the presidents of the Philippines' official summer residence. It was once a summer retreat for Americans. Governing bodies from 1908 until 1935. Although it is obvious that the public is not permitted to reserve rooms at this palace, anyone is welcome to see it. It is lovely to have a glimpse of its majestic palace and expansive, lush garden.

BANAUE RICE TERRACES

Many Filipinos rank the Banaue Rice Terraces as the eighth natural wonder of the world and as a national cultural treasure of the Philippines. The terraces in the Ifugao mountains were almost certainly created by hand. Since more than 2,000 years ago, there have been rice terraces in the hilly areas of Ifugao and northern Luzon Island.

Famous Dishes and Delicacies

POQUI POQUI

This dish, which is notorious for having an uncanny name, is a prime example of making humble ingredients shine with flavor. Made with vegetables like eggplant, tomatoes, and eggs, poqui poqui stands out as a flavorful meatless dish.




LAING

Laing is a Filipino dish composed of dried taro leaves and coconut milk. It was first introduced in the province of Bicol.




IGADO

The name of this dish is derived from the Spanish word for "liver." You can probably guess why-Igado is made with a mix of pork tenderloin and pork liver! Remember to clean the innards thoroughly before cooking this stew.

DINAKDAKAN

Dig into this luscious Ilocano favorite and all its glory: pig ears, pig face, and pig brain in a quick saute with fresh vegetables brings this dish together.

SISIG

Pork sisig is the ultimate Filipino bar chow. Whip it up with a mix of pork belly, pork face, and pork ears for a savory, tasty treat. Level it up with a sizzling plate and an egg!

PANCIT BATO

Originating from the town of Bato in Camarines Sur, this dish is Bicol's version of the popular pancit canton. The noodles used are also yellow and made with egg, but are flatter and thicker.
